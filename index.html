Teaching Inclusion: Evidence Based Strategies & AI Enabled Pedagogy for Restrictive Campus Contexts

SPEAKERS & ORGANIZERS
Donna Maria Blancero (organizer) Erin Kelley Stacy Blake-Beard Mariana Lebron Lakia Rutherford C. Douglas Johnson Alex Hirs
SESSION TYPE
PDW
DESCRIPTION / ABSTRACT
Universities both across the United States and internationally are increasingly adopting policies that restrict or prohibit the use of “DEI” language, programming, or frameworks. Yet the underlying competencies associated with effective DEI education — perspective taking, bias literacy, inclusive decision making, conflict management, and equitable leadership — remain essential for preparing students for contemporary workplaces. This PDW equips instructors in DEI, Organizational Behavior, Human Resources, and Management Education with practical, research-based strategies for teaching these competencies in environments where DEI terminology is discouraged or prohibited. Participants will explore reframing techniques that align DEI related learning outcomes with organizationally neutral or widely accepted constructs such as leadership effectiveness, team performance, psychological safety, ethical decision making, and evidence based management. The session will also demonstrate how generative AI tools can support instructors in designing assignments, simulations, case studies, and classroom activities that foster inclusion related skills without relying on DEI specific language. Examples include AI generated role plays, bias aware decision scenarios, syllabus re-framing, and automated feedback tools for student reflection.

08:30 – 10:30 • Aug 01 PDW

Vignette studies and thought experiments: Innovative tools for management research challenges

SPEAKERS & ORGANIZERS
Jose R. Beltran, Kyle J. Bradley, Ursula Martin, and Herman Aguinis
SESSION TYPE
PDW
DESCRIPTION / ABSTRACT
Vignette studies and thought experiments are effective experimental methods with distinct characteristics and applications that can advance management research across the micro-macro continuum. Vignette studies involve creating scenarios to manipulate variables, facilitating real-time decision assessment, and enabling the exploration of complex phenomena. Despite being underutilized, they have significantly contributed to decision-making, strategic alliances, employee turnover, and leadership. Our Professional Development Workshop (PDW) addresses perceived complexity, making vignette studies more accessible. In addition, thought experiments occur within the researcher's mind, offering unparalleled flexibility in manipulating imagined variables without sample size or participant-related constraints. They are ideal for sensitive topics, particularly ethical and legal issues. Our PDW equips participants with the knowledge to comprehend thought experiments' idiosyncrasies and apply them effectively, transcending specific management subfields. Thought experiments' versatility extends to various management fields, making them widely applicable.

Rooted Resistance III is the continuation of our annual professional development workshop (PDW) on the intersection of marginalization, identity, and self-presentation. 14:00 – 16:00 • Aug 02 PDW

Measuring and maximizing influence: Using the CSII and SIP tools for scholarly excellence

SPEAKERS & ORGANIZERS
Jose R. Beltran, Mauricio Mercado, Bria C. Gibson, and Herman Aguinis
SESSION TYPE
PDW
DESCRIPTION / ABSTRACT
This Professional Development Workshop (PDW) equips scholars with innovative frameworks and measurement tools to elevate their influence beyond traditional metrics. Participants will explore the Contextualized Scholarly Impact Index (CSII). This multidimensional tool integrates contextual relevance, temporal dynamics, and comprehensive data to assess academic impact more accurately than citation counts or the h-index. Through hands-on demonstrations, attendees will calculate their own CSII scores using freely accessible software. The workshop also introduces the Scholarly Impact Performance (SIP) framework, which expands impact across personal, educational, societal, and global dimensions via the CORE performance model (Capacity, Opportunity, Relevant Exchanges). Emphasizing stakeholder engagement, SIP shifts focus from static evaluations to actionable strategies for sustained influence inside and outside of the Academy. In interactive segments, participants will create their own Personal Impact Development Plans (PIDPs) to align skills, activities, and goals with targeted stakeholders and outcomes, fostering communication, collaboration, and adaptability. This PDW provides essential tools to bridge the science-practice gap, inform policy, and drive meaningful change in management education and beyond.
14:30 – 16:00 • Aug 02 Presenter Symposium

Attending to Know-How & Knowing How to Attend: Examining the Mutual Shaping of Attention & Expertise

SPEAKERS & ORGANIZERS
James Rodriguez, David Nicolini, Farnam Mohebi, Nishani Bourmault, Daisy Chung, Yoojin Jeong
SESSION TYPE
Presenter Symposium
DESCRIPTION / ABSTRACT
This symposium examines how attention and expertise are mutually shaped in everyday work. Rather than treating attention as a scarce cognitive resource or expertise as a stable individual attribute, the session brings together practice-based and relational perspectives that conceptualize both as socially produced, materially embedded, and interactionally sustained. Across diverse empirical settings, the papers show how what people notice, ignore, evaluate, and act upon is inseparable from how expertise is enacted, recognized, and transformed. The presentations trace these dynamics across clinical, technological, organizational, and creative domains. The first paper shows how automating peripheral clinical tasks eliminates forms of diffuse attention that are necessary for diagnostic expertise, thereby reshaping what expert judgment can be. The second paper demonstrates how professionals develop awareness of the relational infrastructures that enable their expertise when moving across contexts, and how this awareness reorients what they attend to and how they work. The third paper examines how organizational experts and technology vendors co-construct evaluative standards for AI tools, showing how expertise guides attention toward particular comparisons, risks, and performance criteria, while these attentional practices redefine what counts as expertise in relation to algorithms. The fourth paper analyzes how embodied, sensory attention enables the elicitation and translation of tacit expertise during routine redesign, and how deep routine expertise allows actors to redirect attention away from execution toward change. The final paper shows how animation studio directors use their expertise to organize clients’ attention during feedback on works in progress, shaping how non-experts see, interpret, and evaluate creative work while simultaneously refining their own understanding of client tastes. Together, the papers demonstrate that expertise becomes consequential only when it is attended to, and that attention is never neutral but oriented by expertise, roles, routines, technologies, and relationships. By foregrounding these reciprocal dynamics, the symposium contributes to research on attention, expertise, and organizing by clarifying their interdependence, highlighting the practices through which they co-evolve, and offering methodological and conceptual tools for studying attention and expertise as situated, relational accomplishments.
11:30 – 13:00 • Aug 03 Paper

The Contrapower Tax on Women of Color CEOs: Micropolitics, Emotions, and Organizational Sensework

SPEAKERS & ORGANIZERS
Dr. Samantha E. Erskine and Dr. Diana Bilimoria
SESSION TYPE
Paper
DESCRIPTION / ABSTRACT
Formal authority at the organizational apex does not guarantee epistemic authority—the credibility to define problems, interpret evidence, and set agendas. Through critical grounded theory analysis of 24 interviews with 15 Black women and Latina nonprofit CEOs, we theorize how institutional hegemonies are reproduced and resisted through micropolitical sensework: the situated, power-laden coupling of meaning-making and emotion work that stabilizes or destabilizes legitimacy. We show how predominantly white stakeholders deploy sensegiving and sensebreaking tactics—euphemizing, tone-policing, agenda-resetting, evoking white fragility—to decouple Black women and Latina CEOs' formal authority from epistemic credibility, while those CEOs engage in counter-sensegiving to reclaim interpretive control and sensebreaking to repair sabotaged meaning systems. This process extracts what we term a contrapower tax: the excess emotional, temporal, and political labor women of color CEOs expend managing stakeholder fragility, re-credentialing their expertise, and restoring organizational focus—labor required precisely because they hold authority. We contribute by (1) theorizing micropolitical sensework as the meshing of sensegiving/sensebreaking with discursive emotion work, extending power scholarship beyond cognition to embodied, affective practice; (2) introducing the contrapower tax to explain how formal authority paradoxically increases regulation of women of color leaders; and (3) specifying how the coupling of meaning and emotion orients attention, procedures, and resources to reproduce or contest hegemony at the apex. Our findings advance intersectional leadership theory and reveal mechanisms through which inequality regimes persist even when women of color occupy the top office.
16:45 – 18:15 • Aug 03 Division Paper

Do You Even Know What You Are Talking About? How Accent Bias Impacts Career Outcomes

SPEAKERS & ORGANIZERS
Gabriela Rodriguez, Mauricio Mercado, Scott Moore
SESSION TYPE
Division Paper
DESCRIPTION / ABSTRACT
Implicit leadership theory suggests that leadership evaluations reflect cognitive prototypes of who “fits” leader roles, while implicit stereotype theory highlights how social group cues can automatically activate biased judgments. In hiring contexts, sociolinguistic cues such as accent can therefore function as potent signals of group membership that shape leadership-relevant evaluations. Building on these perspectives, we develop a proposition-based model of how a Mexican Spanish accent influences interview, hiring, and salary recommendations for Latina leadership applicants. Specifically, we propose that a Mexican Spanish accent lowers employability outcomes indirectly by reducing perceived competence via prototype-based lack-of-fit inferences. We further argue that organizational context can disrupt this categorization process: when English–Spanish bilingualism is explicitly stated as a bona fide occupational qualification (BFOQ) in the job description, the accent can be recoded as competence-relevant, attenuating—or potentially reversing—the negative indirect effects of accent bias. By theorizing BFOQs as a boundary condition that can pivot implicit categorization and by centering Mexican women’s intersectional labor-market experiences, this paper extends scholarship on accent discrimination, leadership, and bias mitigation while identifying contextually actionable strategies for more equitable selection.
08:00 – 09:30 • Aug 04 Panel

Beyond Fads and Fashions: How to Produce Relevant, Rigorous, and High-Impact Research

SPEAKERS & ORGANIZERS
Herman Aguinis, David B. Audretsch, Alfredo De Massis, Nicolai Foss, Annabelle Gawer, David J. Ketchen, Christian Kowalkowski, Tatiana Kostova, Wendy Smith, and Silvio Vismara
SESSION TYPE
Panel
DESCRIPTION / ABSTRACT
Business schools, and management scholarship in particular, are facing several existential threats. These include the erosion of perceived relevance, AI disruption, incentive misalignments and publication “games,” the credibility crisis, and excessive theoretical fragmentation and proliferation, among others. Accordingly, producing relevant, rigorous, and high-impact management research is now more critical than ever. The panelists are uniquely qualified to offer specific and actionable advice on how to address these issues in that they are some of the most cited management scholars—those who appear on the Highly Cited Researchers list compiled by Clarivate Analytics-Web of Science. In 2025, only 81 scholars in the broad fields of “economics and business” made the list. Of these 81, only 15 are listed in the Academy of Management’s membership directory (i.e., 18.52%), and 10 of them are on this panel. Although of particular interest to ENT, STR, and TIM, this session will also be of broad appeal given the panelists’ collective membership in 15 AOM Divisions and Interest Groups (i.e., CTO, DEI, ENT, HR, IM, MED, OB, OMT, ONE, OSCM, RM, SAP, SIM, STR, and TIM). Using a question-and-answer format that will include the active participation of the audience, the panelists will address the following topics: (1) Beyond fads and fashions; (2) Relevance: For whom and how?, (3) Rigor: Methods, theory, and transparency; (4) Impact: What, on whom, and how to measure it?, (5) Relevance vs. rigor: Trade-offs or false dichotomy?, and (6) Moving the field forward: Institutions, incentives, and training. The session will offer concrete, experience-based guidance from top-cited scholars that will be useful for AOM members at all career stages.
